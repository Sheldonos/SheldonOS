# Contagious Framework: The STEPPS to Virality

## Overview

This skill provides a structured framework based on Jonah Berger's research to make products, ideas, and content more likely to spread through word-of-mouth. It is built around the six key principles known as **STEPPS**: **S**ocial Currency, **T**riggers, **E**motion, **P**ublic, **P**ractical Value, and **S**tories. 

Use this framework to analyze existing content or to architect new campaigns for maximum contagiousness. You do not need to apply all six principles, but the more you can integrate, the more effective your campaign will be.

## The STEPPS Framework

### 1. Social Currency

**Principle**: People share things that make them look good.

To apply this, craft messages and products that help people achieve desired social images—making them seem smart, cool, informed, or like an insider.

| Tactic | Description | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Find Inner Remarkability** | Make your product or idea unusual, surprising, or extraordinary. Break a pattern. | Blendtec's "Will it Blend?" videos; a $100 cheesesteak. |
| **Leverage Game Mechanics** | Use points, levels, and leaderboards to create status and visible signs of achievement. | Airline frequent flier miles (e.g., Diamond Status); Foursquare mayorships. |
| **Make People Feel Like Insiders** | Use scarcity (limited availability) and exclusivity (access based on criteria) to create a sense of being special. | Invite-only websites; secret menu items; speakeasy bars with hidden entrances. |

**Evaluation Questions**:
- Does talking about this make the user look good?
- What is the inner remarkability? How can we highlight it?
- Can we introduce game mechanics or a sense of status?
- How can we leverage scarcity or exclusivity?

### 2. Triggers

**Principle**: Top of mind, tip of tongue.

Associate your product or idea with cues in the environment that people encounter frequently. A strong trigger is frequent and specific.

| Tactic | Description | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Increase Frequency** | Link your product to a common habit or occurrence. | Kit Kat's campaign linking the chocolate bar to coffee breaks. |
| **Strengthen the Link** | Create a novel but strong association that isn't already cluttered with other meanings. | Mars candy bar sales spiking during the NASA Mars mission. |
| **Trigger at the Right Time** | Ensure the trigger occurs in a context where the desired behavior is possible. | A reminder to brush teeth is more effective in the bathroom than in the car. |

**Evaluation Questions**:
- What is the environmental trigger for this product/idea?
- How often does this trigger occur?
- Is the link between the trigger and the product strong and unique?
- Does the trigger appear in the right context?

### 3. Emotion

**Principle**: When we care, we share.

Focus on high-arousal emotions. It doesn't matter if the emotion is positive or negative; what matters is the intensity of the feeling it provokes.

| Emotion Type | Examples | Effect |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **High-Arousal (Drives Sharing)** | Awe, Excitement, Amusement (Humor), Anger, Anxiety | Activates people and makes them want to take action, including sharing. |
| **Low-Arousal (Inhibits Sharing)** | Contentment, Sadness | Deactivates people and makes them less likely to act. |

**Evaluation Questions**:
- What core emotion does this message evoke?
- Is it a high-arousal emotion?
- How can we amplify the emotionality of the message?

### 4. Public

**Principle**: Built to show, built to grow.

Make the use of your product or the engagement with your idea observable to others. People tend to imitate what they see.

| Tactic | Description | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Create Self-Advertising Products** | Design products that visually demonstrate their use and brand. | The iconic white Apple earbuds; Lululemon's high-quality, branded shopping bags. |
| **Generate Behavioral Residue** | Create tangible artifacts that remain even after the behavior is completed. | "I Voted" stickers; the yellow Livestrong bracelets; Movember mustaches. |

**Evaluation Questions**:
- Can people see when others are using our product or engaging with our idea?
- How can we make private choices more public?
- Can we create a form of behavioral residue?

### 5. Practical Value

**Principle**: News you can use.

People share information that is useful to others. This can include saving money, saving time, or improving health and well-being.

| Tactic | Description | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Highlight Incredible Value** | Make the value of the offer clear and compelling. Use reference points to make discounts seem larger. | Amazon showing the list price next to the sale price. |
| **Apply the Rule of 100** | For products under $100, frame discounts in percentage terms (e.g., 20% off). For products over $100, use absolute dollar amounts (e.g., $50 off). | A clothing store advertising "25% off all shirts" vs. a car dealership offering "$2,000 cash back." |
| **Package Knowledge** | Share expertise and useful information in a clear, concise, and shareable format. | A viral video showing a simple way to shuck corn; how-to guides and tutorials. |

**Evaluation Questions**:
- Does this help people save time, save money, or improve their lives?
- Is the practical value immediately obvious?
- How can we package this information to be more easily shared?

### 6. Stories

**Principle**: Information travels under the guise of idle chatter.

Embed your product or idea into a narrative that people will want to tell. The story acts as a Trojan Horse for your message.

**Critical Rule**: The brand or product message must be **integral** to the story. If you can remove the brand and the story still works, it's a weak integration.

| Tactic | Description | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Create a Compelling Narrative** | Craft a story with a beginning, middle, and end that is engaging and emotional. | Dove's "Evolution" video, which tells the story of a model's transformation into an advertisement. |
| **Ensure Valuable Virality** | Make sure the brand message is central to the narrative, so people can't tell the story without mentioning the brand. | The Panda Cheese "Never Say No to Panda" commercials, where the Panda's presence is the entire point of the story. |

**Evaluation Questions**:
- What is the Trojan Horse story for our message?
- Is the brand integral to this story?
- Is the story interesting enough that people will want to share it?

## Application Workflow

1.  **Analyze the Product/Idea**: Use the six STEPPS principles as a checklist to audit the existing or planned product, message, or idea.
2.  **Identify Strengths and Weaknesses**: Determine which principles are well-leveraged and which are missing or weak.
3.  **Enhance and Integrate**: Brainstorm ways to build in the missing principles. Focus on strengthening at least 2-3 key areas.
4.  **Test and Refine**: Launch the campaign and measure its shareability. Iterate based on the results.
